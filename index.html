The above table is read as follows:

  • Each major row in the table (labeled 1 to 5) contains three characters sharing the same phonetic component.
  • Across each row, the characters from the first two groups of columns (labeled A and B), we see the modern Chinese pronunciations are similar. The upper sub-row shows the Mandarin (普通話); the lower sub-row shows the Cantonese (粵語).
  • To the right of each Chinese pronunciation is the Japanese pronunciation. As you may already know, characters get different readings in Japanese depending, among other things, the time period when they were imported. The upper sub-row gives the Go-on (呉音) variant of the character's readings; the lower sub-row gives the Kan-on (漢音) reading. Wherever Go-on and Kan-on happen to be the same, they are combined into one cell.
  • The Japanese readings may look a bit unfamiliar: they are in historical (etymological) spelling, which makes sound correspondences a lot more obvious.

Observe that the third group of columns (labeled C) has the seemingly exceptional pronunciation in Chinese. For example, in row 1 the characters share the phonetic component 艮, but 根 (cell C1) has the initial consonant g while 痕 (cell A1) and 恨 (cell B1) has the initial consonant h.

Now, most people intuitively understand that pronunciations of characters are likely to have changed since they were first devised hundreds of years ago. But the recurring patterns of similar pronunciations for similar-looking characters seen today would seem to suggest the changes cannot be entirely random for each character. So then, for the ‘exceptional’ characters, were the ancients just drunkenly sloppy in spelling out their sounds? When I was but a schoolkid, I certainly thought so.

If we examine the table more closely, we see that the Japanese pronunciations in column group C also seem to distinguish themselves from those in column groups A and B. This suggests, firstly, that the characters already had diverging pronuncations very early on for them to manifest in both Chinese and Japanese. Furthermore, we can spot the following:

  • In row 1, that the Japanese Go-on has a voiced consonant g in columns A and B but not in column C, so whatever difference the pronuncation turns out to be in ancient times might just have something to do with voicing and de-voicing.
  • In row 2, the pronunciation of all three characters seems ‘regular’ enough in Mandarin, all having vowel a; and so does the Japanese. Cantonese is the odd one out in that we have the contrasting vowels oe and o. Yet, if we look at the Japanese, what is distinct about character C is that it is spelled with no y but characters A and B are. Perhaps the ancient pronunciation only differs in the y sound which might explain the phonetic component 昜 being the same.

  • The same thing happens in row 3, where column A and column B sound different enough already — with Japanese taking two different consonants s and t even. Most native-language speakers (in any of the three languages) probably do not realize the graphical component 者 occurring in 都 even indicates any phonological value like in the characters 者, 豬 (and quite a few others like 緒, 署). I certainly did not until I read [Dǒng 2008, p. 41–42]!

    This particular series of characters is also discussed in [Xiàng 2023 Ⅰ, p. 81 & 102].

  • In row 4, Japanese has consonant r corresponding to l in Chinese, naturally. Both the Japanese r and the Chinese l disappear at the same time for the exceptional character. But the vowel in Japanese does not really differ between the three characters, suggesting that 藥 has phonetic component 樂 because the two characters sounded closer than in modern Chinese dialects.
  • In row 5, Japanese gains a consonant d or t to distinguish the character from column C. Under Go-on the vowel is a on all three characters, but changes under Kan-on except for character C, suggesting characters A and B were diverging further from C (particularly, with respect to the vowel) in the time period between when Go-on and Kan-on got established (and thus long after the three characters got devised).

So, with scant knowledge of what ancient Chinese sounded like, by just comparing the reflexes from just three modern languages, we have been able to partially uncover the mysteries surrounding these characters and their phonetic components. This, I hope, convinces you that our investigation will prove interesting and fruitful. Once we explore ancient Chinese phonology more, we will soon find that all the observations we have made are no mere coincidences but have logical explanations.

2. What is Middle Chinese?

Middle Chinese 中古漢語 represents the stages of Chinese presumed to have been spoken roughly between 200 AD to 1300 AD, from the start of the Three Kingdoms period (after the Han dynasties) to the Song dynasties (before the invasion of China by the Mongols). Informally, we may treat it a language like any other in history, which can be subjected to linguistic study, e.g. using the comparative method. But technically speaking, Middle Chinese is a constructed system of pronunciation, which has been passed down to us through written records of philologists from that era and since.

We know, from the written record, that fairly divergent dialects of Chinese even existed back then, so certainly no one could be said to have spoken a single language called "Middle Chinese". However, what linguists have been able to deduce is that varieties of Chinese had evolved from a proto-language with the following characteristics:

  • Its spoken form was primarily monosyllabic: not only everyday words, but even vocabulary that we would consider rather specialized today, like names of species of plants and animals, consisted of one syllable. [Xiàng 2023 Ⅳ, §2]
  • Only later were multi-syllabic words incorporated into everyday language, often replacing old monosyllabic words as the latter became too ambiguous. Not only because the limits on the number of distinguishable phonological features were hit, preventing the coining of new words with new syllables, but also individual monosyllabic words were increasingly overloaded with many shades of meaning, or even completely new meanings, as society evolved to articulate new concepts. [Xiàng 2023 Ⅳ, §3]
  • It had a distinctive written form consisting of "characters", whose modern, evolved form are recognizable to many people today.
  • Each character was pronounced as one syllable. For monosyllabic words that means one character corresponds to one word. For compound words made of a series of monosyllabic morphemes, the compound word would be written with the corresponding characters to those monosyllabic morphemes. Genuinely multi-syllabic morphemes (usually disyllabic) would still be written with multiple characters even if though the characters may have no meaning outside of being part of the multi-syllabic morpheme.
  • The above key aspects of the language are retained to this day in modern Chinese dialects.
  • Many of the phonological changes in varieties of Chinese could be analyzed syllable-by-syllable — though not all, of course. So we can effectively speak of a Chinese character having certain a "pronunciation" at a certain time and place, and how it changes throughout history across different dialects.

The proto-language that Middle Chinese evolved from is called Old Chinese 上古漢語. Old Chinese is way beyond the scope of this article, and its re-construction is subject to considerable uncertainty. But knowing some aspects of its evolution is still useful, in shedding light on certain pecularities of Middle Chinese. We will point out some of the salient details as we go along.

3. Primary sources on Middle Chinese

We give a brief overview of the major kinds of data we have on Middle Chinese.

  • Rhyme dictionaries, which list out groups of characters that sound the same or similar. As the term implies, one of the purposes of rhyme dictionaries were to help ancient poets write rhyming poetry.
  • Rhyme tables, which organize all the syllables of the language systematically in two-dimensional tables, analogously to how we do the same today for Mandarin Chinese (e.g. [Pīnyīn 2008]).
  • The ancient poetry whose verses are supposed to rhyme, thus giving clues to pronunciation.
  • Other dictionary-like and expository texts written in ancient times.

For the moment, let us contrast the situation of Middle Chinese versus a Western language written with an alphabet, say Ancient Greek. Even though the sounds assigned to various letters may change over time, and the spelling of words may become fossilized (i.e. retaining etymological spellings that do not reflect current pronunciation anymore), intuitively nobody seriously doubts a fairly direct connection between how words are written versus how they sound.

But because Chinese, until modern times, had never employed phonetic writing in a systematic manner, one may reasonably wonder how much value do Chinese historical records give. To that point, the evidence from the four categories of works mentioned so far can be fairly indirect. The saving grace is that ancient Chinese philologists learned to analyze their language as ancient India did to Sanskrit. (Though, as one might expect, ancient terminology and notation were nowhere as precise and unambiguous as in modern linguistics.)

Finally, we have data that can be subject to the comparative method like linguistics applied to any other language:

  • Pronunciations or readings of characters in sino-xenic languages and Modern Chinese varieties.
  • Phonetic transcriptions of known foreign terms/loanwords into Chinese characters.

To give an example that everybody can recognize, the word buddha was, and continues to be to this day, phonetically approximated by the character 佛. From this fact we may surmise that this character was originally pronounced with a / b- / initial consonant, despite it being pronounced today as fó in Mandarin and fat⁶ in Cantonese, though it is indeed pronounced as butu (仏) in Japanese.

It is mentioned that at the beginning of the Kāihuáng 開皇 era (AD 581–600) of the reign of Emperor Wen of Sui, there was a small gathering hosted by 陸法言 (Lù Fǎyán), where discussions about correct pronunciations came up. They noted that the dialects at that time had considerable phonological differences, and a standard or correct pronunciation system was need by scholars and their students.   1. ▲ Some authors merge 娘 into 泥, on the belief that they were not pronounced any differently, at least by the time the 廣韻 was written. Nevertheless it can be confirmed in the present day there remains a phonemic distinction, in that the 反切 from 廣韻 form two equivalence classes for 泥 and 娘.

  2. ▲ Early re-constructions of Middle Chinese determine these initials to be (alveolo-)palatal stops 舌面塞音. The consensus seems to have settled on these being retroflex, after considering they might have emerged from a medial *r from Old Chinese. The philological term 舌上音, literally meaning "above-tongue sound", is itself ambiguous on this matter.

  3. ▲ 齒上音 is a relatively modern appellation for these initials. In other texts, this row may simply be called 正齒音, mixing it up with the next row for the alveolo-palatal sibilants. 正齒音 was the original Chinese philological term, but only in the 19th century did 陳澧 (in his pioneering work 《切韻考》) discovered that, what had been called 正齒音 consisted of two sets of initials. Later investigations revealed that the initials in this row (i.e. 齒上音) were likely retroflex in the same way as 舌上音.

  4. ▲ The consonants are definitely grouped very oddly in this row: we elect to do so to make the table more compact, at the same time adhering to established convention from Chinese philology. From the modern point of view, / x /, / ɣ / are clearly velars, while / j / is a palatal glide. (On the other hand, 喉音 literally means "throat sounds".) The details are too complicated to get into here, but initials referred to by 匣, 云, 以 had gotten mixed up historically because of sound mergers, explaining why they all ended up being categorized as 喉音. However, they are distinguishable as (three) equivalent classes in 反切 from the 廣韻. Many authors, in the modern context, categorize these initials into the respective rows for velars and palatals.

  5. ▲ 日 is often categorized into the row for 正齒音, as those initials there were also articulated with the palate.

    Occasionally, one may still encounter a phonetic description like / nʑ / for the 日 initial. That is plain wrong as a sound value, because consonant clusters were already lost by the period of Early Middle Chinese, but is meant to suggest that it is the precursor to the retroflex that later developed in northern Chinese varieties (e.g. r- in 普通話 which can be pronounced as / ʐ /), while southern Chinese varieties might have preserved the nasal pronunciation.

    The philological term 半齒音, literally "half-tooth sound", was probably meant to suggest this initial did become a sound like / ʐ / in later Chinese varieties, as opposed to 半舌音 (below) meaning "half-tongue sound".

    The reader may notice too, interestingly enough, in Japanese this initial similarly became z-.

  6. ▲ 來 is often categorized into the row for 舌頭音.

7. Bibliography

In writing this article, I have consulted sources in all three languages, English, Chinese and Japanese. Each language source has unique viewpoints to offer, and all have been a pleasure to read.

  • Most of the English works below, usually aimed at students of Sinology or Chinese linguistics, are comparatively easy to understand. They use modern linguistic terminology and notation that you may already be familiar with from other languages. And they take care to explain background supporting information, such as the meanings of any cited texts written in Classical Chinese.

    Even without consulting native-language works, the English texts already give a good survey of Old & Middle Chinese and their features. They also more readily draw connections with other languages spoken in areas bordering China, e.g. tonogenesis was first established in Vietnamese by Western linguists, and later Old Chinese was postulated to have undergone an analogous process.

  • The works in Standard Chinese add more flair and detail, with copious evidence from classical literature. They are especially good at tracing each historical change going from Middle Chinese onwards.

    Yet, undoubtedly because of the political unpopularity of the topic, the information contained therein on non-Mandarin dialects tend to be an afterthought. Fortunately, there are many reference works for specifically Cantonese (owing to Hong Kong's history and culture) that can fill the gaps.

  • Then, we have works in Japanese authors. Naturally, they excel at connecting Chinese phonology with concurrent and subsequent changes in the Japanese language. While Chinese and English writers all acknowledge the role played by sino-xenic readings in Chinese linguistics, the information they offer on Japanese tends to be superficial — nothing that advanced learners of Japanese could not deduce themselves after learning a bit about Middle Chinese. Understandably, Japanese is a difficult enough language that few non-Japanese could be expected to analyze at a high academic level.

    On the other hand, a good number of Japanese sinologists are world-renowned in their own right. And they do sometimes bring up important connections in Middle Chinese phonology that other authors neglect, e.g. how the difference in medials under divisions 3 and 4 manifest in modern languages today (notably Mandarin, Japanese and Korean).

Most of the non-English works cited here do not have official English titles. For the benefit of the reader I have supplied my own translations. If you want to search for a work yourself, please look it up under the original non-English title.

Some works here, particularly dictionaries, overlap greatly. Nevertheless, I have listed them all as authors on this article's topics do occasionally disagree, if not in opinion or perspective, then in terminology and notation, and so had to be cross-checked.
